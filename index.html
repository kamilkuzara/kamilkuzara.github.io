Parallelising JavaFF

I completed the project during my penultimate year at university. As it was a substantial individual project, it was the equivalent of a Bachelor's Thesis. The topic of the project was "Introducing parallelism to the JavaFF planner" and the goal was to analyse the sequential algorithms used by JavaFF - a Java-based implementation of a popular planner FF - and propose parallel alternatives.

The project involved a significant amount of research across the field of automated planning. Looking into the relevant literature, I investigated possible solutions to the problem and gained an in-depth insight into the area of classical planning as well as a broad overview of the entire field. Despite the wealth of existing research in the area of parallel planning, I did not find a solution that would be feasible to implement into the JavaFF planner without the need to redesign the entire system. Therefore, I proposed 3 solutions of my own. They parallelised various aspects of the search.

I am especially proud of the Parallel Enforced Hill-Climbing Search algorithm of my invention as it successfully sped up the search. In fact, on some test problems, my algorithm turned out to be almost 50% faster than the standard, sequential version. Developing the algorithm required the use of various concurrent programming techniques such as barriers, mutexes, and a sophisticated deadlock prevention system. The other solution of my design that performed well was a parallelisation of the computation of heuristics. Albeit simple conceptually, it required me to redesign the sequential search algorithm to accommodate the use of a thread pool. The added effort proved to be worth it as the solution was also successful in speeding up the search.

Working on this project allowed me to learn advanced concepts in concurrent programming and parallel algorithm design. I also got to dive deep into the field of automated planning and AI and was able to appreciate the complexity of this research area.

Pintro

Pintro is a mobile networking app. Similarly to LinkedIn, its fundamental objectives are to enable connections in a professional and business setting. The project was developed by a team of seven as part of a university coursework. We were tasked with developing the app for an external client. The functionality of the app includes features such as user and business profiles, and a fully fledged real-time chat.

The system we developed is based on a client-server architecture. The client app (or the front-end, if you will) was developed in React Native and the server (back-end) was based on Node.js, Express.js framework and MongoDB. This fit in the "JavaScript everywhere" philosophy. The testing of the system was performed with Postman and Jest, and we used Docker for deployment. The choice of all these technologies was guided by their flexibility and allowed for rapid development. In fact, it enabled us to complete the project in time and to client's high expectations. Moreover, of all other teams' projects, it was ours that was chosen for deployment by the client.

Most of my work was at the back-end. I was involved in the modelling of the database. Then, I developed REST API endpoints which enabled interaction with the model. It was important that the system supported secure connections and log-in functionality. Therefore, I took up the task of implementing some fundamental security measures. Specifically, I implemented the HTTPS server ensuring security of data in transit, as well as strong password hashing using the PBKDF2 function with salt. I also worked at the front-end developing app components such as the chat.
